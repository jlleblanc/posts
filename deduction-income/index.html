Deduction Income

There's money we all spend on essential things, such as housing, food, higher education, clothing, transportation, healthcare, and retirement. In the United States, you can deduct some of this off of your income taxes, but this can be difficult.

Rather than give people deductions for spending money on things, why don't we give everyone the money up front, tax-free?

Give everyone the same benefit

As of 2020, the IRS contribution limit for 401k retirement accounts is $19,500. While this limit is not increased every year, it is increased in $500 increments often enough that we can assume it will someday soon be $20,000. (This also turns it into a nice round number.)

When you contribute this money, it comes out of your paycheck as a pre-tax benefit: that is, it is tax-free income for the year. This is great if you have an employer offering 401k accounts and $20,000 to contribute to the account. If you don't have both of these, it's pointless: you're not getting a benefit while wealthier people are. Furthermore, even if you have access to and means to contribute to a 401k, you have to actively make the contribution. If you don't contribute, you don't get the benefit.

Instead of requiring active participation in a 401k plan, we should give every adult $20,000 a year tax-free in monthly installments. Doing so would ensure many things:

  • Everyone would get the benefit. Without a means test, everyone would be getting $20,000, no questions asked. This would ensure everyone benefits, not just those who can afford to.
  • The deduction is built-in. By receiving the money tax-free, everyone effectively gains a deduction. There is no need to write money off that you're contributing to a retirement account, since you're receiving the money underneath anything you've earned.
  • The benefit will increase over time. Since everyone is receiving the money, there is political support to increase the benefit to match inflation.

What can the money be spent on?

Anything. This is money that can either be spent on immediate needs or invested for retirement. However, the idea is to have $20,000 that can be spent on needs at different stages in life. Consider these scenarios:

  • Turning 18. The monthly cash starts arriving when you turn 18. With this money, you can do the following:
    • Pay for college
    • Pay for non-college post-secondary education (e.g., apprenticeship, trade school, career boot camp, etc..)
    • Find a home to split with roommates
  • Finding a first job. Once you have finished with your post-secondary education, you have some cash on hand to live off of while you look for a first job.
  • Saving for a first home. Once you start your first job, you can then save or invest your money so you can put together a down payment on a first home.
  • Invest for retirement. Since the money is already tax-free, you can invest it in a retirement account.
  • Childcare. Parents of young children can use this tax-free money to pay for childcare while working.
  • Unemployment. If you lose your job or otherwise lose income, you are guaranteed this money. No need to prove that you have lost your job.
  • Retirement. Once you retire, you continue to receive this money tax free.

How would we pay for this?

A flat income tax applied before deductions. For most people, this would effectively move a portion of their earnings to the untaxed deduction income.

The tax rate would be determined by the number of adults in the country and the total dollar amount of income earned by the entire country:

(Adults * Deduction Income amount) / Total Income

We can use 2019 numbers to calculate this:

Number of adults over 18: 255,042,110
Proposed Deduction Income amount: $20,000
Total deduction income to pay out: $5.1T
Total US Income 2019: $18.6T
Tax rate needed to support Deduction Income: 27.5%

Under this scenario, the breakeven income level for Deduction Income is $72,727. That breakeven point will rise and fall depending on the tax rate, which depends on the grand total of US personal income and the total population. Anyone making less than $72,727 would be getting more money from Deduction Income than they're paying in, while anyone making more than this would be contributing towards everyone else's Deduction Income. Regardless of personal income, the Deduction Income would always be tax free.

Isn't this just Universal Basic Income?

Yes. However, most UBI proposals are for arbitrary amounts (like $1000/mo) and rely on revenue streams other than income tax (e.g., mineral rights, wealth taxes, etc...) This proposal uses income tax to make it clear where the money is coming from. It also ensures the program is not debt-funded. Finally, it is based on the amount allowed for tax-free retirement contributions, which will increase over time to account for inflation.
